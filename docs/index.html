I'm of the belief that there are two main groups of researchers interested in the g-ratio value. One group are interested in the g-ratio as an optimal ratio that nature found to optimize conductivity in axons, the same way other conducting cables & surrounding insulator have an optimal ratio. But a second group appears to be mostly interested in using the g-ratio as a unitless/dimensionless measure that quantifies the myelin thickness. Meaning, they are using the g-ratio as a substitute for a myelin thickness measurement for different disease models, regardless of axon caliber. My concern is that some of the technical choices in measuring the g-ratio may impact the interpretation or physical reality differently. For example, one could image taking a circular axon, and stretching it infinitely long along one axis while retaining the overall axon and fiber area. In this case, the area-based g-ratio stays the same, but 1) the myelin thickness would clearly be much lower, and 2) even though the g-ratio is the same, this non-circular geometry would likely change the conductivity because now there is much less distance between the inner axon and the myelin. In a less extreme example where the axon is simply deformed from a circle but is irregularly shape, the myelin thickness likely doesn't change substantially, but the conductivity could have changed (electrical simulations would likely demonstrate how important this effect is, but I haven't done these). What I'm trying to get at is, when we are looking for defining a g-ratio in a non-ideal geometry (e.g. not concentric circles), are we looking for the g-ratio to retain its meaning in terms of the myelin thickness estimate, or in terms of how close/far it is from the ideal conductivity from an ideal axon with a g-ratio ~ 0.6?
